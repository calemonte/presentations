What is Digital Scholarship?

use of digital evidence and method, digital authoring, digital publishing, digital curation and preservation, and digital use and reuse of scholarship.

- Abby Smith Rumsey (2011)

The ARL endorsed definition is relatively dry. It's true, but I find the "digital use and reuse of scholarsip" unsatisfying.

A not-quite-pin-downable collection of skills, methods, and approaches for answering scholarly questions using digital technology, often involving community building.

  • I like this defintion that I made up better. It gets at the fluidity of this field and it's relatively amorphous qualities.
  • Micah Vandegrift said that "Generally digital scholarship resists attempts to be corralled into any one field, thereby serving as a bridge across disciplinary divides. It's hard to pin down and has so many different permutations across organizations." That's true, and you see it in the variety of services, specialities, and focuses for different groups.
  • I also like this definition because it reveals a bit about the human element of all of this -- that it's about collaboration, about building a community of researchers and practioners, and how we often share the outputs of our work very broadly and publicly.

What are the most common methods in DS right now?

The methods that we commonly see under the umbrella of digital scholarship include some of the following (and I'm going to provide some examples because those make things more interesting). It should be obvious that not every DS unit is going to specialize in every single one of these. Universities should focus on the areas of strength and where their abilities are greatest.
Mapping & GIS
Geographic information systems (GIS) is the combination of tabular data (e.g. a spreadsheet) with geographic boundaries (e.g. maps).There are all kinds of examples, but a couple that I want to highlight.
American Panorama -- a project of the Digital Scholarship Lab at the University of Richmond -- is an historical atlas of the United States for the twenty-first century. It combines cutting-edge research with innovative interactive mapping techniques, designed to appeal to anyone with an interest in American history or a love of maps.
Penn Libraries Map Room
Mapping and GIS is also manifested in participatory experiences that involve people in the creation of scholarship and understanding.
Data Curation & Database Design
  • Data curation is the organization and integration of data collected from various sources. This work involves annotation, publication and presentation of the data such that the value of the data is maintained over time, and the data remains available for reuse and preservation. In other words, digital curation is stewardship that provides for the reproducibility and re-use of authentic digital data and other digital assets.
  • Again, I'm turning towards another Penn Libraries DS project, Data Refuge, which exemplifies this part of data stewardship and reproducibility.
Visualization
How can we understand data? How can it be visualized in ways that are compelling and revelatory in some way?
Here's an interesting one that demonstrates the type of works this entails. It's Geoff Boeing's visualization of the street network orientation. It allows you to appreciate something in a new and interesting way.
Network, Statistical, and Textual Analysis
These are compuational approaches to quantifying and exploring relationships between entities, such as people, places or things, or textual elements in a novel. Very often displayed in some sort of visualization or graphical interface.
Network of "Viral Text" Sharing, 1836-1860 This graph illustrates connections among pre-Civil-War newspapers from the Library of Congress' Chronicling America newspaper archive and Making of America magazine archive. The circles (nodes) in the graph represent individual newspapers or magazines, while the lines between them (edges) represent shared texts discovered by researchers working on the Viral Texts project, sponsored by Northeastern University's NULab for Texts, Maps, and Networks.

How are these methods delivered?

Project-based collaborations and partnerships with faculty, centers, student groups, and others
  • Much of this work is built around producing some sort of output – an interactive map, a digital monograph, and publicly accessible database of information.
  • It’s also work that deeply integrates the library employee in the research process. It means working side by side the researcher to create something new or interesting.
Consultations (one-off and ongoing)
Consultations are relationship building enterprises and really the bread and butter of DS services. Consultations on the topics that we listed above, but also on the sort of meta-practices that sometimes get glossed over: Project management support, training, and involvement. Workflow creation and providing support in really thinking through the actual work of a project, sharing redflags and experiences from other areas. Much of this work relates to setting expectations and molding something into an achievable end product.
Workshops, trainings, and events for a wide variety of audiences
Community building, bringing together different areas of the university
Facilities and hardware/software provision
This one's obvious, but not all DS units have facilities. Server space, deployable technologies like Omeka, Scalar, WordPress/Drupal are common.
In-course instruction and partnerships in pedagogy
Tiered/pyramid model is common organizing principle for services
Generally speaking, you see many DS units approaching the delivery of their services by having tiers or levels.
  • At the top is the highly-collaborative, longer term projects.
  • In the middle is the bread and butter type of stuff (consultations, workshops, email correspondence).
  • And the bottom is the systematized stuff (for instance, the IR collections or an out of the box Omeka site).

How is DS administratively organinized?

Distributed collection of research practices and services
  • Amorphous collection of practices that doesn’t necessarily have a physical instantiation (a webpage that collects disparate activities). More like a cross departmental/functional unit that offers services from various people in the library system.
  • dSHARP from Carnegie Mellon University is self-described as “a collection of research practices using web-facing tools and methods that generate research products with online interaction or consumption as required modes of use.” They don’t do any of the web development or technical support that some of other projects might provide, but they consult and provide a locus for that type of work.
Centers/departments without a footprint
Centers that don’t have facilities but are nonetheless more administratively organized than those cross-functional units. I’d say that’s what we have had up till this point.
Centers/departments with a footprint
Physical centers with facilities for consultations, meetings, and other sorts of interpersonal connection:
  • Emory Digital Scholarship Center (huge)
  • University of Alberta Digital Scholarship Centre (launched this past summer and not as crazy, but still nice) - https://dsc.library.ualberta.ca/
  • Doesn't need to be this elaborate though! Columbia’s Studio at Butler is a single room that started off pretty modestly. The point is that there's a space that's recognizable in which you can base programming around, host events, and normalize the presence of DS.
Labs
  • Labs are like hybrid blends of these more traditional seeming centers with state-of-the-art technologies. It's like a hybrid of what we have in WIC with much more. The video looping in the background is from the new(ish) Digital Scholarship Lab at Michigan State. It's crazy.
More focused around a specific set of research topics (text analysis or data mining).

What are the challenges facing DS?

Keeping things focused while also capacious
There is a way array of methods and approaches in DS. I spent a lot of time going through all of the different local permutations of methods and appraoches that DS _can_ be, and despite that it can still be a lot to wrap your head around.How do you focus on a coherent set of services / areas of focus while also remaining flexible and open to new techniques?
Supporting wide range of disciplinary needs
The various methods intersect with every discipline, but sometimes it's a challenge to meet the unique needs of every discipline. This isn't not a new challenge to librarianship, but it's something that affects DS right now.
Limited time and resources to spend on custom projects
There is always a tension between providing out-of-the-box services for publishing or digital exhibits, but there's often desire on part of the project partner to implement custom development or other forms of customization. The challenge is using the resources and skills at our disposal to provide high-quality services to our communities while not overextending ourselves.

Despite the variety of DS flavors, what are the common defining features?

Regardless of the physical instantiation, there are certain commons features of Digital Scholarship right now that you see regardless of the methodologies supported, facilities operated, etc. These are heavily informed by a great article that I read by Micah Vandegrift on the various ecologies of DS.
Typically project-based
Project based, but service-infused (ie, projects are the tip of the spear but there are a host of services that are easily accessible if you ask). This goes back to the pyramid model. Projects are the most high profile type of work that DS -- and it's a field that will always have a heavy tendency to embrace projects -- but orientation towards providing routine services.
Strives for open by default
DS as a field leans towards openness, open access for the outputs and means of producing those outputs. Interested in publishing for consumption beyond the academy – public engagement.
Predisposed to new research methods and technologies
Technology-dependent and generally very interested in innovative ideas, new tools, new methods for answering questions.
Platform/tool agnostic
Related to technology-oriented, not necessarily tied to any one technology or tool. The platform is not the solution -- just a tool to solve a problem. It shares this with software development in many respects.
Interested in lifecycle issues
DS tends to be interested in research lifecycle issues – metadata creation, project management, and publishing/distribution.
Highly collaborative and oriented towards partnerships
  • It’s a field that partners with wide variety of groups and is based on meaningful collaborations between researcher and librarian/staffer. Often serves as connective tissue between different groups, connecting patrons/partners with resources, tools, and techniques.

What is the future of DS?

So, this leads me to thinking about the future of DS. I'm not great at prognosticating the future, but I think that all of these common features serve as foundation underpinning what is to come and influences my thinking. I also want to focus on what I think _could_ be the future here at Penn, as opposed to the future more generally.
Entrenched presence across university
  • Normalize and entrench the presence of DS in the university. There's a recommendation to institute a center. Facilities create presence; space matters). Focus on interactive spaces where students, faculty, and others are encouraged to physically connect around technology and tools. The idea is to normalize the digital scholarship department/center as a location where people can come for big collaborative projects, small one-of helping things, and ongoing regular series of workshops on popular topics.
  • Caveat here is that a center can silo off activity (center while also remaining somewhat de-centered). The downside is that a distinct Center can be isolating, it puts the activities of this group in a box distinct from the traditional library activities. The future is where we have entrench existing partnerships, established new dotted line connections with liaisons in every discipline, and effectively normalized the presence of DS in the univeristy through our parnterships, services, and publications.
  • I think the future also is one in which we more systmetically integrate DS' outputs and infrastructure in the internal workings of the Libraries. Essentially, stronger integration between DS work and broader library system. For instance, how do we include the outputs of digital scholarship in the collection development activities of the libraries? How do we meaningful preserve the outputs of these projects? Duture of DS is an integreated approach to the outputs and maintenance of these materials in the Library system.
Community-building as increasingly significant activity of DS units
I think there will be more and more focus on intentionality in community building. The idea that digital projects aren’t merely technical collaborations but social enterprises that engage whole communities around ideas, methodologies, and areas of study. I think it's also about creating a broader community of practice, of shared interests in technology and new technologies, of trying to create experiences and understanding that is current and expansive in all of the ways that technology affords (but also recognizing the materiality of life and the essential roles libraries play in a familiar sense). Hub of activity and learning for a broad array of people. I think public programming as well is important. What can we do to engage with the community of developers out there? What about the general public? That relates to my next thought.
Focus on research outputs with impact beyond the university
DS as externally engagement -- Emphasize community outreach and building beyond the academy in permanent positions. We live in an age where academics _need_ to translate their research for the greater good, and the community outreach + relationship building component needs to be intentional and emphasized.
Further integration with the research process/lifecycle
There is only going to be further integration with the research process; continue to see DS as a co-creator in the research process, valued advisor on projects and research initiatives.
Reflective of trends in technology and modes of scholarly expression
Reflective of trends in technology and modes of scholarly expression (VR/AR, machine learning, data science, wearable technologies and other internet connected devices).
Still true to its defining features
Building on #5, I think DS is going to continue to be a field that is platform-agnostic and prone towards experimentation (while still being entrenched in the system and no longer seen as "new"). Set up structures and workflows that allow for experimentation and quick pilots. Don’t belabor the process through overly elaborate approval process. If a tool is going to be used, discuss the merits and red flags with the partner. Hold events to whet appetites and encourage understanding. If kernel of idea emerges, triage it. The process of pushing things down the pyramid of experimental to service to rote is essential to this work.
